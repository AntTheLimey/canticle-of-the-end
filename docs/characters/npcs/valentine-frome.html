Description

Twenty-nine. Three years in Bengal. The face is what people see first and cannot stop seeing. A tulwar took his left ear and most of the cheek below it during a skirmish near Cawnpore. Badly stitched by a regimental surgeon who was drunk and in a hurry. The scar tissue pulls his mouth into a permanent half-smile on the left side, exposing teeth. One eye sits slightly lower than the other where the orbital bone healed wrong. He keeps what remains of his hair cropped brutally short because anything longer catches in the scar tissue.

The body beneath is lean, efficient, maintained. He moves like a man who has been hit many times and learned exactly how far he can move to avoid being hit again.

The Contrast

He reads Horace in the original. Speaks fluent Hindustani and passable Bengali, learned from his men, not a munshi. Writes in a small, precise hand. His journal entries alternate between meticulous documentation of Thuggee patterns and brief, beautiful observations about the Hooghly at dawn. He plays the flute, badly, and his men claim to love it.

His sepoys call him “Sweet Valentine” without irony: a term of genuine affection. He remembers their families, visits their sick, writes letters home for the illiterate. In the British mess the same nickname carries a different weight: mockery first, then unease, because the man it belongs to is clearly more competent than anyone using it as a joke.

Background

Wrote a report about ritualised killings among sepoys in the Baranagar district: strangulations with a specific ligature pattern he recognised as Thuggee method. His commanding officer told him to drop it. The scarred face made it easy to dismiss. He stopped writing reports and started keeping a private journal instead.

These were his men. When they disappeared or turned up strangled, nobody cared because they were Indian soldiers and the British assumption is that “native quarrels” explain everything. Frome couldn’t accept that, not because he’s sentimental, but because the pattern was obvious to anyone who looked and nobody would look.

Personality

Still, watchful, economical with words. When he speaks it tends to be worth hearing. Drinks little. Reads alone while others play cards. Not ambitious. The scarred face gives him social permission to be solitary; he uses it. Underneath: furious. Not at his disfigurement. At the indifference.

Motivations

Justice for his dead men. Someone to finally listen. He does not need convincing that something is wrong. He needs allies who will act.
