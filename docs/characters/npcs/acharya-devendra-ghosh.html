Ghosh is the Acharya of the Mandir Mahakali at Baranagar Ghat, on the western bank of the Hooghly north of Calcutta. He is the leader of the Aeternum_Choir’s Calcutta cell, and his written voice combines the authority of a man at home in sacred tradition with open contempt for his European counterparts. He does not seek Herzfeld’s approval. He informs him.

His letter to Albin_Herzfeld carries the particular confidence of someone who regards comparison with European occult practice as faintly insulting. “Your European methods of concealment are fragile. Ours are not. The British see a temple. They do not see what the temple contains.”

Combat

Ghosh does not fight. If physically attacked, he does not resist. If cornered and alone: Wrack on the nearest attacker, then Dominate on whoever hesitates. If capture is imminent, he begins chanting, invoking a partial dissolution field (Dissolution Touch on himself, spreading outward). Suicide by transcendence.

Spells

Dissolution Touch (custom) — 1D6 MP, 1 round. Physical contact + chanting. POW vs POW against unwilling targets. Affected area partially dissolves for 1D6 rounds (1D4 HP, 1D6 SAN to witness). Resolidification is imperfect.

Amplify Svarita (custom) — 4 MP, sustained. 3 rounds prep. Harmonises with svarita carriers, dramatically amplifying the “tone between tones.” Activates the yantra. Scales to encompass the ghats. Disrupting Ghosh’s concentration collapses the amplification.

Wrack (reflavoured) — Variable MP. POW vs POW. Inflicts intense pain, 1D6 temporary CON loss, all skills halved while affected. “The pain of separation from the divine.”

Dominate (standard) — Variable MP, 1D6 SAN. POW vs POW. Target follows one command per round for 1D6 rounds.

Consecrate Rumaal (reflavoured Bless Blade) — 4 MP, 10 minutes. Consecrated silk cord: +1D4 damage, supernaturally silent strangulation. Lasts until dawn.

Contact Yog-Sothoth (reflavoured) — 1D6 SAN, 8 MP. 1 hour meditation in the inner sanctum, in contact with the yantra. Receives impressions of the Canticle’s global progress. Performed approximately once per month.

Ritual: The Cycle of Dissolution

Major ritual requiring the yantra, 5-7 svarita carriers, Ghosh sustaining Amplify Svarita, proximity to the river, and hundreds of genuine mourners providing the base harmonic. 4 hours from evening cremation chants to full dissolution. Effects begin subtly at hour 2, visible at hour 3, catastrophic at hour 4.

Disruption: Any TWO of these together prevent the catastrophic outcome: destroy/damage the yantra, silence 3+ carriers, break Ghosh’s concentration, physically disrupt the river channel.

Background

Ghosh’s family line has served at the Mandir Mahakali since what he calls “the first utterance of the name that opens the gate” — a phrase implying his cell’s work predates the Choir’s European operations by centuries, or that it was an original site rather than a recruited one. He claims the Calcutta cell’s traditions predate European civilization by three thousand years.

The Mandir Mahakali sits at Baranagar Ghat, north of Calcutta on the western bank of the Hooghly. As a functioning temple, it provides cover that Ghosh regards as unbreakable: the British colonial apparatus sees a place of Hindu worship. It does not see the ritual infrastructure beneath or behind that fact.

The yantra, the ritual instrument specific to the Calcutta cell’s working, was complete at the time of writing. Singers have been selected from those whose throats carry the svarita, the tone between tones. The ritual is planned for 25 October, Kali Puja, in the month of Kartik.

Motivations

Ghosh’s letter does not contain doubt. There is no anxiety about the timeline, no request for reassurance, no curiosity about the state of the Vienna operation beyond what is strictly relevant to coordination. His tradition has waited. It will perform. The Grand Canticle will proceed.

His contempt for European methods is not hostility toward the Choir’s goal. It is confidence that his approach to the same goal is superior: rooted in unbroken tradition, disguised inside living practice, and executed with ritual precision that requires no mechanical apparatus.

[!info] Keeper Only Ghosh’s overconfidence is characterized and functional. The British colonial administration does, in fact, fail to look twice at the Mandir Mahakali. His methods have worked for a long time. Whether that overconfidence is the vulnerability the investigators ultimately exploit is a Keeper decision, but it is the most obvious angle. A party that arrives with Occult, Anthropology, or History knowledge of the Sanskrit terminology in his letter (yantra, svarita, sadhana, pralaya, smashan) will have a meaningful advantage in understanding the ritual’s mechanics and the cell’s vulnerabilities before direct confrontation.

Appearances

Personality

The voice: Warm, measured, genuinely interested in people. Asks about your family, your health, your spiritual life. Remembers details. Offers tea. The kind of priest who makes you feel heard. He means it. He also means to dissolve you into the river.

The conviction: Has experienced dissolution personally (his left hand, beneath the priestly wrappings, is boneless and plastic). He knows what it feels like. He found it transcendent. He believes he is offering the greatest gift: freedom from the prison of individual identity.

APP 65 at sixty-two reflects the uncanny quality that extreme conviction produces. Beautiful in the way saints in paintings are beautiful: ageless, serene, radiating certainty. People trust him on instinct. That instinct is correct in every context except the one that matters.

His reaction to being stopped: Accepts it with the patience of a tradition that has survived three thousand years. The Cycle will come again. Not afraid. Not angry. Disappointed.
