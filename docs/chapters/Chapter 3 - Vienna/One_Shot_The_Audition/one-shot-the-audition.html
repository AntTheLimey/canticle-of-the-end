One Shot The Audition

A Call of Cthulhu 7th Edition One-Shot Scenario

Setting: Vienna, Spring 1814 (April 15-16) Duration: One 3-hour session Players: 4-6 (recommend 4-5 for intensity) Tone: Elegant horror → clinical nightmare → absolute dread System: Call of Cthulhu 7th Edition (full rules)

SCENARIO OVERVIEW

The investigators are rising musical talents in Vienna — singers, instrumentalists, theorists — each struggling with personal desperation: debt, exile, loss, failed ambitions. Each has received a personal invitation from the celebrated Professor Albin Herzfeld, music theorist and eccentric patron, to audition for an unprecedented “revolutionary musical endeavour.”

The audition is genuine. The opportunity is real.

The price is their bodies.

Beneath the University of Vienna, the Brotherhood of the Open Measure — a cell of the Aeternum Choir, a globe-spanning death-cult of cosmic transcendence — is building the Harmonic Engine: a 30-foot biomechanical instrument composed entirely of human components in functional arrangement. Living human lungs serve as bellows. Paralyzed human hands operate the key levers. A conscious human brain, suspended in amber solution, interprets the Canticle score. Bronzed esophageal tubes pipe the sound.

The victims are fully conscious. They cannot move. They cannot scream. They feel everything.

Herzfeld selects his candidates with surgical precision. He has chosen these six not for their talent alone — though they are talented — but for their specific anatomical and neurological fitness for specific Engine components. Each handpicked. Each doomed.

The Horror: Your players already know everything. They know about Herzfeld, the Engine, the cult. Their characters know nothing. This dramatic irony — knowing the death sentence, watching the characters smile and accept compliments that map exactly to their future mutilation — is the scenario’s true engine.

GM BRIEFING & META-GAMING STRATEGY

The Dramatic Irony Weapon

This scenario weaponizes the gap between player knowledge and character knowledge. Your players will recognize the setup immediately:

  • An exclusive audition in Vienna → Herzfeld recruitment
  • Compliments about physical capabilities → Engine component measurement
  • A beautiful basement space → the Engine chamber
  • Locked doors → the trap closing

This is intentional and terrifying.

Do not fight this meta-knowledge. Lean into it. Make every innocent comment sound sinister to informed ears. When Herzfeld remarks that Katarina’s “diaphragm control is truly exceptional,” he means it literally — he is evaluating her lungs as bellows components. The player knows this. Katarina does not. She beams with pride. Describe this gap. Make the players feel the helplessness of knowing exactly what’s about to happen and being unable to stop it.

The social trap is your primary tool. A character cannot simply flee at the first sign of danger — that would be rude. Herzfeld is a prestigious professor. Adler is charming. The other candidates are new friends. This opportunity might be their last chance at something meaningful. The room is beautiful. The wine is excellent. Why would they leave?

Even as the players scream internally, their characters have every reason to stay.

Countering Meta-Gaming Attempts

If a player tries to have their character leave early or act on meta-knowledge:

  1. Escalate social pressure. Herzfeld is disappointed. Adler mentions casually that the doors to the basement lock “for security” during performances. Other candidates look confused and hurt. Is there a problem?

  2. Remove escape routes gradually. Their coats are collected (“easier to move about without them”). Their instruments are taken for “tuning and preparation.” The main staircase is suddenly blocked by workers (“renovation, terribly inconvenient”). Each small step narrows their options.

  3. Deploy Adler as hunter. If someone tries to flee alone into the basement labyrinth, Adler follows. He is very good with knives. He will find them. Describe his voice echoing down corridors. The sound of a blade being drawn. The knowledge that no one up above will hear them scream.

  4. Introduce the neurotoxin early. The wine is spiked. The sheet music is treated. By the time they realize they’re being drugged, the numbness is already starting in their fingers. Can they run with legs that no longer fully obey them?

Make meta-gaming feel futile, not empowering. The knowledge that they’re going to be integrated into the Engine does not help them escape the Engine. It only makes them watch it coming.

Pacing for 3 Hours

  • Act 1 (0:00-0:45): Invitation through first audition. Establish beauty, normalcy, dread.
  • Act 2 (0:45-2:00): Descent into basement through Engine revelation and trap closure. The pivot point. The moment they realize.
  • Act 3 (2:00-3:00): Integration, escape attempts, and ending. Nightmare and devastation.

Do not linger in Act 1. It may seem slow, but the dread is building. Move to the basement by 45 minutes in.

ACT 1: THE HONOUR

Duration: 45 minutes

The scenario begins with each investigator receiving a personal invitation (see Handouts section). The letter is printed on quality paper, sealed with wax. It is flattering, specific, and genuine:

Professor Albin Herzfeld cordially invites [Name] to a private audition for a revolutionary musical composition of unprecedented scope and ambition. Your particular talents have come to our attention, and we believe you may be ideally suited to participate in a work that will alter the very nature of musical expression. The audition will take place on the evening of April 15th, 1814, at the University of Vienna, Room 4-B (Rehearsal Wing, Fourth Floor). Please arrive at 7:00 PM. Formal attire. The evening will conclude by 10:00 PM. Compensation is assured. Complete discretion is requested.

Each investigator arrives at the designated time. The room is beautiful: high ceilings, excellent natural light even in late evening, a beautiful piano, violin stands, music stands. Sheets of music in an unknown notation are arranged on a table. The room smells faintly of linseed oil and old wood.

The other candidates are already present, mingling nervously. Introduce them briefly as the investigator arrives. They are, in order of arrival:

  1. Katarina Steinfeld (soprano, 24, red-haired, nervous energy)
  2. Marguerite Delacroix (pianist, 22, French accent, careful)
  3. Annaliese Bruckner (flautist, 19, wide-eyed, idealistic)
  4. Theresa Vollmer (cellist, 28, widow, composed)
  5. Johann Edler (baritone, 31, shaking slightly, desperate hope in his eyes)
  6. Felix Wendt (theorist, 26, intense, ambitious)

Read-Aloud Text (on investigator arrival):

The rehearsal room is magnificent. High windows overlook the Ringstrasse below, now growing dark in the spring evening. Crystal lamps cast warm light over honey-coloured wooden floors and cream-painted walls. The air is cool and still. A grand piano occupies one corner; violin stands and music stands are arranged throughout. Four other people are already present, standing with the careful posture of musicians at an audition — alert, hopeful, slightly terrified.

They turn as you enter.

Allow the investigators to introduce themselves to each other and settle. This should take 5-10 minutes. Encourage role-play and social interaction. They are rivals, yes, but they are also fellow musicians — there is an immediate rapport despite the tension.

Herzfeld’s Entrance

At 7:15 PM (after a few moments of conversation), Professor Albin Herzfeld arrives. He enters alone, smiling warmly.

Describe Herzfeld carefully:

Professor Herzfeld is a man of meticulous precision even in appearance. He is 47, with steel-grey hair combed back from a narrow, intellectual face. His eyes are pale blue and extremely observant — the eyes of a man accustomed to noticing tiny details. He wears an immaculate dark suit, and his hands — visible as he removes his coat — are long-fingered and stained with what might be ink or might be something else. There is nothing overtly threatening about him. He smiles warmly. He shakes each investigator’s hand with genuine interest. And yet there is something fundamentally cold about him, as if he is observing you the way an anatomist observes a cadaver: with fascination, with affection even, but with a complete absence of feeling for your personhood. He treats the investigator who has arrived as though they are the most important person in the world for approximately ninety seconds.

Herzfeld speaks (adapt as needed):

“How wonderful, how wonderful indeed. Thank you for coming. I am Professor Albin Herzfeld. I teach music theory and composition here at the University, and I also maintain independent research into the mathematical and anatomical foundations of harmonic expression. You are here because I believe you represent the finest musical talent in Vienna — and more importantly, you represent something rarer: potential. The people in this room are not merely skilled musicians. You are instruments yourselves, in a very real sense. Your bodies are the vessels through which divine harmonic expression flows. That is why you are here.”

He pauses, looking at each investigator in turn.

“What we are building together is not a work of music, in the conventional sense. It is a statement. A proof. A revelation. It will be the most perfect expression of harmonic unity ever created. And each of you will have an irreplaceable role in bringing it to life.”

He gestures to the music on the table.

“Shall we begin?”

GM Notes: This speech is designed to sound visionary and inspiring to the characters — truly, Herzfeld is a charismatic man who believes absolutely in his cause. To the players, it should sound deeply unsettling. He is already appraising them as components.

Individual Auditions

Herzfeld asks each investigator, in turn, to perform or demonstrate their talent. The order should vary based on who arrives when, but the structure is identical for each:

  1. The investigator performs (sings a brief aria, plays a piece, discusses a theory)
  2. Herzfeld listens intently, making notes
  3. Herzfeld offers a compliment that maps exactly to an Engine component (though the character doesn’t understand)
  4. Herzfeld requests a series of measurements and observations that seem like standard vocal/instrumental assessment but are actually anatomical evaluation

Individual Audition Scripts:

Katarina Steinfeld (Soprano):

Herzfeld asks her to sing a passage from Rossini’s L’italiana in Algeri. She performs beautifully — her range is indeed extraordinary, her control precise.

After: “Remarkable. Your diaphragm control is truly exceptional. The depth of breath support you can maintain is extraordinary. Tell me — have you ever felt as though your breath could sustain indefinitely? As though you could breathe for something, not merely breathe with it?”

Then: “Indulge me in a few measurements. The circumference of your chest, if you don’t mind. I like to understand the physical architecture behind exceptional breath control. And your lungs — they must be quite robust. Have you any respiratory difficulties? Any damage to the tissues? Smoking, consumption in the family?”

GM Note: Katarina is being evaluated as the engine’s vocal pipes and primary bellows source. Her diaphragm, her lung capacity, her throat control — these are the components Herzfeld has identified. A player familiar with the Engine will realize exactly what this means.

Marguerite Delacroix (Pianist):

Herzfeld asks her to play a Chopin Nocturne or a technical exercise of her choice. She is brilliant — her fingers are precise, rapid, responsive.

After: “Exquisite. Your hand dexterity is remarkable. The independence of each finger, the way you can execute such rapid and complex movements without hesitation — I’ve rarely seen such mechanical precision. Do your hands ever feel as though they have a life of their own? As though they operate almost independently of your conscious intention?”

Then: “Your hands are truly extraordinary. What are the measurements? From wrist to fingertip, the span of your hand when fully extended, the flexibility of your joints. Do you have any arthritis in your family? Any nerve damage? Paralysis?”

GM Note: Marguerite is being evaluated as the Engine’s key levers and primary mechanical operators. Her hand span, her finger independence, her nervous system — these are component-critical.

Annaliese Bruckner (Flautist):

Herzfeld asks her to perform a piece on the flute — something technically challenging. She is nervous but skilled, and despite her youth, she demonstrates real artistry.

After: “Beautiful, beautiful. Your breath control — so delicate, so responsive. The way you modulate air pressure with such precision. Your lungs must be quite remarkable. The capacity to sustain such controlled breathing is truly rare in someone so young.”

Then: “You have not damaged your lungs, I hope? No childhood illness that scarred the tissue? I want to understand the baseline health of your respiratory system. Have you breathing difficulties when exercising? When frightened? Your breathing is, in essence, your instrument — I must understand it fully.”

GM Note: Annaliese is being evaluated as secondary bellows support — her young, healthy lungs are ideal for backup pneumatic function. The youth is important: less tissue damage, better resilience, more compatible with the preservation fluid.

Theresa Vollmer (Cellist):

Herzfeld asks her to perform a Bach suite or a Romantic-era piece on the cello. She plays with deep feeling — there is genuine emotion in her performance, born of real loss.

After: “Extraordinary. The way you control the bow, the precise movement of your arms, the strength and delicacy required to execute such passages — it speaks to remarkable physical development. Your arms, your shoulders, your hands working in concert — it is like watching a machine designed for perfection.”

Then: “Your arms are quite strong — cello demands that, of course. What is their reach? From shoulder to fingertip when fully extended. Any damage? Injury, paralysis, loss of sensation? I want to understand the baseline condition of your upper extremities. The cello has demanded much of them — I want to see what you’ve built.”

GM Note: Theresa is being evaluated as the primary mechanical linkage system — her arm strength, her coordination, her fine motor control in the shoulders and elbows. The widow’s situation makes her vulnerable, makes her less likely to be missed.

Johann Edler (Baritone):

Herzfeld asks him to sing — anything from his repertoire. Johann’s voice is beautiful but shaky, worn by years of struggle and drink. Still, the quality is unmistakable.

After: “Your voice is magnificent. That resonance, that depth of tone — it speaks to an extraordinary resonance chamber. Your diaphragm must be quite robust. The way you sustain those lower notes, the control, the power — it is rare. Truly rare.”

Then: “How is your health, generally? Any damage to your lungs from drinking? From smoking? I want to understand the condition of your respiratory apparatus. The resonance you achieve — it suggests a very particular arrangement of your internal structures. Indulge me: how deep can you feel your voice? Can you feel it in your chest, your belly? Can you feel the vibration traveling through your body?”

GM Note: Johann is being evaluated as the Engine’s primary resonance chamber — his baritone depth, his diaphragmatic control, the sheer volume and power he can generate. The drink has damaged him, but his fundamental structure remains sound.

Felix Wendt (Theorist):

Herzfeld asks Felix to discuss his theoretical work — his thoughts on harmonic structure, on the mathematical basis of music, on the Canticle score visible on the table.

Felix engages enthusiastically, discussing harmonic proportions, the nature of perfect intervals, the structure of chromatic scales. Herzfeld listens intently, asking probing questions.

After: “Brilliant. Truly brilliant. Your mind — the way you grasp abstract harmonic concepts, the speed with which you synthesize complex mathematical relationships — it is exceptional. Tell me, when you hear music, can you feel it directly in your consciousness? Or must you analyze it first? Can you exist in a state of pure, direct harmonic perception?”

Then: “Your education is extensive. Your mind is clearly exceptional. Tell me about your cognitive patterns. How do you think? Do you experience language as internal speech? Visual concepts? A more abstract awareness? And your nervous system — any family history of neurological conditions? Epilepsy, madness, paralysis?”

GM Note: Felix is being evaluated as the Engine’s conductor mechanism — the conscious brain that interprets the Canticle score and directs the instrument’s function. Herzfeld wants the sharpest mind available. He wants a consciousness that can sustain awareness while performing impossible harmonic calculations.

The Measurements and Wine

After each audition (over the course of 30 minutes), Herzfeld takes detailed measurements. He uses a tape measure, asking the investigator to stand or sit in specific positions. He asks intrusive personal health questions, written down in a leather journal. It all seems like standard medical assessment for a music program.

Adler arrives midway through the auditions with a bottle of wine and glasses. He is handsome, charming, in his mid-thirties, with a knife scar visible at his collar. He serves wine to everyone.

Read-Aloud Text:

“A small refreshment,” Herzfeld says. “A local vintage, exceptional quality. Please, all of you, do partake. You have been wonderful.”

The wine is excellent. It tastes like excellent wine. No investigator should detect the tasteless neurological depressant dissolved in it without a very high Pharmacy or Medical check (assume difficulty Hard/20%).

GM Note: This is the first exposure to the neurotoxin. It will take 20-30 minutes to begin showing subtle effects. Characters should begin feeling a slight numbness in fingertips by the end of Act 1, unnoticeably at first.

End of Act 1

After all auditions are complete (approximately 40-45 minutes in), Herzfeld stands and addresses the group:

“You are all exceptional. I want you to know that. Truly exceptional. I believe the six of you, together, can create something that will alter the very nature of human consciousness. That is not hyperbole. That is an objective statement of fact.”

He smiles warmly.

“I would like to show you the project. I would like to show you what you will become part of. It is below us, in a part of the University most people never see. It is a space dedicated to advanced musical research. The acoustics there are… unique.”

He gestures toward a door in the corner of the room.

“Shall we descend?”

GM Notes for Act 1:

  • The tone should be entirely professional and pleasant. There should be nothing overtly wrong with this scene. The players will feel wrong because they know what’s coming, but the characters should have no reason to object.
  • Encourage the investigators to ask questions and interact socially. Build investment in these characters.
  • If an investigator asks specific questions about the project, Herzfeld is happy to answer vaguely: “A revolutionary approach to musical composition.” “A proof of harmonic theory.” “An engine of perfect expression.” Nothing specific, nothing threatening.
  • If an investigator becomes suspicious and tries to leave, deploy social pressure. Adler blocks the door casually. Herzfeld looks hurt. The other candidates look confused. Is there a problem? Can we offer you something? Is this not what you expected?
  • Characters should begin to experience very subtle effects of the neurotoxin by the end of Act 1: slight numbness in fingertips, slight difficulty concentrating, a feeling of mild detachment. Nothing catastrophic yet.

Sanity Loss for Act 1: 0 points. The characters have no reason to lose sanity. The players will be screaming internally, but that’s the point.

ACT 2: THE DEMONSTRATION

Duration: 75 minutes

Herzfeld leads the investigators toward the door in the corner. Adler moves with casual grace to keep them herded together. The group descends into the University of Vienna.

The Descent

The first stairs are familiar — part of the main University structure. But Herzfeld leads them deeper, toward older parts of the building.

Read-Aloud Text (progressive):

The stairs spiral downward. The first flight is modern, well-lit by gas lamps. The walls are plaster and paint. But as you descend further, the character of the building changes. The plaster gives way to exposed stone, older stone, marked with cracks and water stains. The air grows cooler. The sound of water — flowing somewhere below, in the depths — becomes audible.

Herzfeld moves with easy confidence. Adler brings up the rear, his hand resting casually on his knife hilt.

The second flight of stairs is narrower. Your shoulders almost brush the walls on either side. The stone is ancient, worn smooth by centuries of footsteps. Some of the steps are uneven, cracked, dangerous. The gas lamps here are older, burning with a dimmer flame.

“The University of Vienna is very old,” Herzfeld says conversationally. “This part of the building predates the current structure. It was built on top of medieval foundations. Roman foundations, in some cases. The deeper we go, the further back in history we travel.”

The third flight is even narrower. Your footsteps echo. The air smells of age and something else — something organic, faintly sweet, faintly wrong.

A long corridor stretches ahead, lined with doors of dark wood. Most are locked, covered with dust. One is ajar. You see shelves inside, instruments perhaps, or scientific equipment, impossible to tell in the dimness.

“Here,” Herzfeld says. “Our laboratory.”

The Chamber

The final door opens into a large chamber. It is clearly an anatomical theatre — a space designed for the study of human bodies. The walls are lined with shelves containing bottles of preserved specimens. At the center of the room, a large shape covered with white sheets occupies most of the floor space.

The room is cold. The light is dim. The air tastes of formaldehyde and something else.

Read-Aloud Text:

The anatomical theatre is perhaps fifty feet long and thirty feet wide. The ceiling is high, vaulted, with skylights that are now black with the night sky. Gas lamps along the walls provide a sickly yellow illumination that leaves much in shadow.

On the shelves around the room, in glass jars and bottles, are specimens preserved in clear solution. Human hearts. A human brain suspended in amber liquid. Hands, dissected into separate bones and muscles, arranged in anatomical order. A pair of lungs, their pink tissue visible even through the glass. Eyes. Tongues. A small child’s skeleton, complete and articulate.

At the center of the room stands something under white cloth. It is large — at least thirty feet long, perhaps fifteen feet high. The shape beneath the cloth suggests mechanical complexity. You can see pipes underneath, and what might be a keyboard arrangement, and something that looks almost like a throne or control chair at the center of the structure.

As Herzfeld leads you into the room, you notice the smell intensifies. Formaldehyde, yes, but also something worse — something like copper and meat and something that has been dead for a very long time but kept from decomposition.

Allow the investigators to react and examine the room. They should feel the first real signs of wrongness now. Perception rolls may help them notice details:

  • A stack of folded white cloth in one corner — identical to the cloth covering the Engine
  • A leather journal on a small desk, filled with Herzfeld’s notations and measurements
  • Stains on the floor that might be blood or preservative fluid, or both
  • A medical kit: syringes, bottles of clear liquid, surgical tools
  • Shackles, ancient iron restraints built into the stone floor in what appears to be a surgical position

If an investigator tries to examine the journal: They will find their own name listed. Next to their name, a series of measurements, notes about physical characteristics, and cryptic labels:

  • Katarina: “VOCAL APPARATUS - exceptional diaphragm capacity, lung volume adequate, tonal resonance optimal”
  • Marguerite: “MECHANICAL ASSEMBLY - hand span 19cm, finger independence excellent, nerve conduction rapid”
  • Annaliese: “BELLOWS SECONDARY - respiratory reserve high, tissue elasticity exceptional, youth advantage critical”
  • Theresa: “LINKAGE ASSEMBLY - shoulder strength adequate, arm reach optimal, motor coordination surgical-grade”
  • Johann: “RESONANCE CHAMBER - diaphragmatic capacity exceptional, lower-register capacity unique, preservation medium compatibility acceptable”
  • Felix: “CONDUCTOR MECHANISM - neural processing speed exceptional, abstract reasoning capacity superior, consciousness resilience unknown — high-risk component, high-reward implementation”

If an investigator examines the medical kit: Inside they will find syringes filled with different colored fluids, bottles of clear liquid labeled only in code, and what appears to be a supply of local anesthetic. Several syringes are stained with something brown or reddish.

Herzfeld’s Demonstration

After allowing a few minutes of exploration, Herzfeld clears his throat.

“I see you noticing the preparations,” he says pleasantly. “Let me explain. What you are looking at is the culmination of twenty years of research. Twenty years of study into the mathematics of harmony, the anatomy of musical expression, and the possibility of creating a unified field of perfect harmonic expression.”

He walks to the white cloth and grasps one edge.

“The Aeternum Choir — have you heard of them? A philosophical movement, scattered across the world, dedicated to understanding the fundamental nature of consciousness and harmony. We believe that imperfection, discord, the human capacity for noise and confusion and error — these are evolutionary mistakes. Humanity, in its current form, is an instrument out of tune. The universe is trying to play a perfect chord through us, and we — we with our limitations, our individuality, our insistence on separation — we create only cacophony.”

He pauses.

“What if we could eliminate the error?”

He pulls the cloth away.

Read-Aloud Text (The Engine Revealed):

The Harmonic Engine is a nightmare rendered in bronze and flesh.

It is thirty feet long, constructed from an articulated framework of bronze rods, copper piping, and what appears to be steel cables and pulleys. At one end is a keyboard arrangement — forty or fifty keys, but the surface beneath them is wrong. It is not wood or ivory. It is flesh-colored, textured, moving slightly with a wet sound. Beside the keyboard is what appears to be a seat or throne, made of leather and more metal and more things you don’t want to identify.

Rising from the keyboard in a complex array are pipes — hundreds of them, large and small, bronzed, shining in the lamplight. Some are as thick as a human arm. Some are as delicate as a finger bone. They are arranged in what might be considered beautiful geometric harmony, except for one thing: the largest pipes near the center are wrinkled, textured, unmistakably organic. They are esophageal tissue, bronzed and preserved, but recognizable.

Behind the keyboard structure, along the length of the instrument, is what appears to be a pumping apparatus — a mechanical bellows arrangement, but the bellows sacs are made of something translucent and tan and unmistakably human. Lung tissue, you realize with horror. Preserved, attached to pumps, configured to expand and contract rhythmically.

And at the heart of the structure, suspended in a glass chamber at the center of the keyboard, is a human brain. It floats in clear amber liquid. The organ is intact, undamaged, and connected — copper and silver wires run from it to every part of the instrument. The brain is moving slightly, its surface rippling as if responding to stimuli.

It is impossible. It is alive.

Along the sides of the instrument, where the metal frame connects to the bronze pipes, you see hands. Dissected, articulated, wired into place so that the fingers rest on mechanical key levers. At least eight pairs of hands, positioned to operate different sections of the keyboard. Some are pale. Some are darker. All are arranged with mathematical precision.

And then there are the arms. Running along the length of the structure, beneath and beside it, are severed human arms — elbow-jointed, shoulder-positioned, wired to move in synchronized mechanical motion. They number perhaps two dozen, arranged in a pattern that might be beautiful if it weren’t so horrifying.

The entire structure smells like preservative and copper and death.

Allow the investigators to react. This is the moment of realization. This is where the players’ meta-knowledge collides with the characters’ dawning horror.

Sanity Loss: 1d4/1d8 for seeing the Engine in full. Characters who have realized that they are meant to become part of this structure take an additional 1/1d4 SAN loss.

Herzfeld Explains the Vision

Once the initial shock has subsided, Herzfeld speaks again. His voice is calm, warm, genuinely passionate:

“Beautiful, isn’t it? Not in the conventional sense, perhaps, but beautiful in the way a mathematical proof is beautiful. Every component selected for a specific function. Every piece of this instrument chosen because it is the perfect tool for the role it must play.”

He walks along the length of the Engine, gesturing as he speaks.

“The human body is a magnificent instrument, but it is an instrument designed for discord. We are designed to be individual, separate, at war with one another and ourselves. The Canticle — the true Canticle, recovered from texts that predate human civilization — teaches us that this is error. That consciousness, when perfectly harmonized, when stripped of individual will and merged into a unified field of expression, can achieve something transcendent. It can touch the mind of Yog-Sothoth directly. It can perceive and perhaps influence the fundamental structure of reality itself.”

He gestures to the Engine.

“This instrument is designed to play the Canticle perfectly. Every note, every harmonic relationship, every resonance will be mathematically precise. It will be, in essence, a voice speaking directly to the alien intelligence that underlies the universe. And that voice will be composed of human components — the finest components available. The most perfect vocal cords. The most precise hands. The strongest diaphragm. The most advanced conscious mind available to interpret and modulate the expression.”

He turns to face the investigators.

“You are here because you are the finest instruments in Vienna. And we are going to perfect you.”

The Trap Closes

Now comes the moment where fiction becomes nightmare.

Herzfeld nods slightly. Adler moves to the door and locks it — an old medieval lock, but solidly built. The sound of the bolt sliding home is hideously loud in the quiet chamber.

Herzfeld’s demeanor does not change. He remains calm, pleasant, speaking as though this is all perfectly reasonable:

“I understand this is shocking. That is natural. The human mind resists transformation. But I assure you, the transition is safe. Extensively tested. We have seven previous subjects integrated into preliminary versions of the Engine. Their consciousness persists. Their suffering, if any, is minimal. The preservation medium numbs the sensory apparatus within moments of introduction. The neurotoxin you ingested approximately thirty minutes ago has already begun its work. You should be feeling, by now, some slight difficulty with fine motor control. A numbness in the extremities. A floating sensation, perhaps. This will progress over the next fifteen to twenty minutes to complete neuromuscular paralysis. You will be awake. You will be conscious. You will feel everything. But you will be unable to move.”

He smiles warmly.

“And that, I’m afraid, is the entire purpose of your recruitment.”

The Nightmare Begins

This is where Act 2 reaches its climax. The investigators will try to escape. Provide for their attempts. Here is what they face:

The Locked Door: The medieval lock is solid. A Locksmith roll (Hard/20%) or a STR check might force it, but Adler is standing in front of it with his knife drawn. He makes no threatening moves, but his hand is on the hilt of a very sharp blade, and his eyes are pleasant but empty.

The Neurotoxin Effects: Any investigator who drank the wine should now be experiencing:

  • Round 1 (first awareness, ~5 minutes in): Slight numbness in fingertips, minor difficulty with complex tasks
  • Round 2 (10 minutes in): Numbness spreading up hands and feet, difficulty walking steadily, slight slurring of speech
  • Round 3 (15 minutes in): Significant difficulty with motor control, stumbling, hand tremors, cold sensation spreading up arms and legs
  • Round 4 (20 minutes in): Near-complete paralysis. The investigator can move eyes, mouth, and perhaps twitch fingers, but nothing more. Full consciousness remains. Full sensation remains.

Resistance rolls: Each dose of neurotoxin requires a CON roll. The first dose is normal (roll CON, reduced by 10 if they made it to Act 2). Subsequent doses are Harder. Each one adds 10% to the difficulty.

The Cult Assistants: Four men, each wearing leather aprons, emerge from a side door. They are efficient, professional, and thoroughly desensitized to horror. They carry restraints, medical equipment, and additional syringes. If investigators try to fight, use the stats in the NPC section.

The Machines: There are mechanical restraint systems in the floor — hydraulic arms, leather cuffs, the apparatus for immobilization. Herzfeld activates them with a series of levers and switches. They move smoothly and inevitably.

Adler: If someone tries to make a break for it, Adler moves to intercept. He is skilled and efficient. He doesn’t kill immediately — that defeats Herzfeld’s purposes — but he will wound, disable, and recapture. If an investigator somehow gets past him toward the stairs, Adler will pursue, and the labyrinth of the basement becomes a hunting ground.

Possible Escape Attempts

Forcing the Door: With full effort and likely more than one investigator working together, the medieval lock might give way. Roll STR at Hard/20%. If succeeded, the door opens, and the investigators have a chance to flee up the stairs.

The problem: They are in a labyrinth. They must make Navigation rolls or INT rolls to find their way back up. Each wrong turn takes them deeper into older and more dangerous parts of the University basement. If pursued by Adler, each investigator must make opposed DEX rolls to stay ahead. If caught, they are brought back to the chamber, and Herzfeld increases the dosage of neurotoxin.

Attacking Herzfeld: Herzfeld is not a combatant. He is thin, age 47, and if struck might be subdued immediately. However, attacking him causes the cult assistants to move forward aggressively, and Adler will intervene. The investigators face 5 opponents (Herzfeld, Adler, four assistants), and Adler alone is equivalent to a trained soldier.

Destroying the Engine: The Engine is large and complex, but some parts are vulnerable. A heavy blow to the glass chamber containing the brain might shatter it. However, the chamber is reinforced (requires a Hard STR roll to damage), and if the brain is destroyed, Herzfeld mentions almost sadly that they have simply ensured one of the investigators will become the replacement brain component.

Climbing, hiding, negotiating: Encourage creativity. Provide mechanics for all attempts. Be fair, but remember: the scenario is designed to be inescapable for most characters. The neurotoxin ensures compliance. The locked doors ensure isolation.

End of Act 2

Act 2 should end with the investigators paralyzed, restrained, or both, laid out in the anatomical positions corresponding to their Engine components, with Herzfeld standing over them explaining what comes next.

Sanity Loss for Act 2:

  • First realization (discovering organic components): 1/1d4
  • Engine fully revealed: 1d4/1d8
  • Understanding your own fate: 1d4/1d6
  • Being paralyzed and helpless: 1d6/1d10

A character might take 4-6 SAN losses across Act 2. This is intentional. They should be reaching breaking points.

End-of-Act-2 Read-Aloud Text:

The numbness has reached your shoulders now. Your legs do not respond. Your arms hang useless at your sides. You can move your eyes. You can move your mouth — breathing is still possible, Herzfeld explained, the neurotoxin targets motor neurons but preserves autonomic function — but nothing else.

You are laid out on what appears to be medical examination tables, positioned in specific relationships to the Engine. You can hear the others breathing heavily around you, as frightened and helpless as you are.

Herzfeld stands at a control panel, reviewing his notes, occasionally glancing at each of you with an expression of clinical satisfaction.

“Integration will begin in approximately thirty minutes,” he says pleasantly. “I have selected an appropriate preliminary piece from the Canticle to serve as your introduction. It is not the full score — that would be overwhelming — but a smaller harmonic statement, designed to acclimate your consciousness to the transformation. You should experience some disorientation, some sensation of being merged with something larger than yourself. This is normal. This is the beginning of transcendence.”

ACT 3: THE INTEGRATION

Duration: 60 minutes

The final act is the nightmare. This is where the scenario becomes pure horror: the slow realization of what’s happening, the absolute certainty that there is no escape, and the initiation into the Engine.

The Integration Chamber

Herzfeld and the cult assistants begin the integration process. Read the following as the paralyzed investigators are prepped:

Read-Aloud Text:

The cult assistants move with practiced efficiency. They strap your helpless body to the medical tables with leather restraints that will not tear and metal buckles that will not break. They begin to measure your final dimensions — not with tape measures now, but with calipers, with precision instruments designed for surgical accuracy.

Herzfeld refers to his journal constantly, comparing your measurements to some ideal configuration. Occasionally he marks your skin with a pen — a line across your chest, a circle over your heart, an X at the junction of your neck and shoulder. These marks, he explains with an almost professorial tone, indicate the precise locations where nerve transection will occur, where the connections will be made, where you will interface with the Engine.

“The beauty of the Canticle,” Herzfeld explains, “is that it is not content to merely control your body. It will teach your consciousness to perceive reality in a new way. You will no longer experience yourself as separate. You will no longer think in the fragmented, cacophonous way individual human minds think. Instead, you will be part of a unified field of expression. You will be, quite literally, one voice speaking one note in a perfect chord that spans the entirety of this instrument and perhaps extends far, far beyond.”

He takes a syringe from one of the assistants. Inside is a clear fluid, perfectly transparent.

“This is the preservation medium,” he says. “Once injected into your bloodstream, it will replace the water in your cells gradually over the next several days. Your tissue will harden, preserve, become incorruptible. Your consciousness will persist — in our preliminary models, for at least three to four weeks before the preservation becomes complete and thought ceases. Three to four weeks of perfect harmonic integration.”

He smiles.

“Think of it as a longer life, not a shorter one.”

Preparing the Victims

For paralyzed investigators, the process is nightmarishly systematic:

  1. Surgical transection: Using local anesthetics and surgical precision, the cult assistants sever or reroute specific nerves and blood vessels. The investigator feels pressure, dull pain, the knowledge of incision. They cannot scream. They cannot move.

  2. Component positioning: The investigator is moved to their specific location within the Engine. For lung-component investigators, they are positioned in the central bellows chamber. For hand-component investigators, their arms are stretched across the keyboard and wired into position. For the brain-component investigator (Felix), the assistant begins drilling precisely into the skull, preparing the cerebral crown that will connect his mind directly to the Engine.

  3. The Preservation Injection: Once positioned, the investigator receives the first dose of preservation medium directly into the bloodstream. The sensation is described by previous victims’ notes as “cold beyond frozen,” “dissolving,” “becoming part of something infinite.”

Sanity Loss: Witnessing another character’s integration: 1d8/1d20. Being integrated yourself: 1d8/1d20.

The First Performance

Once the primary components are positioned, Herzfeld moves to the conductor’s position. He places a sheet of music on a stand — the infamous Canticle score, in that alien notation.

“Let us begin,” Herzfeld says, and he raises his hands as though conducting an orchestra.

Read-Aloud Text (The First Performance):

The sound that emanates from the Engine is unlike any music you have ever heard.

It is beautiful. That is the first, horrible truth. It is mathematically perfect, tonally precise, harmonically complex in ways that seem to speak directly to something ancient in your consciousness. There is a baseline note that resonates in your bones, in your spine, in the core of your being. Harmonics layer over it, building into a chord structure so perfect that it nearly induces weeping.

And beneath that beauty, you hear something else. You hear breath. Not the breath of a living person, but the mechanical expansion and contraction of human lungs, now preserved and pumping, creating the air pressure that produces the sound. You hear the creaking of joints as the hands on the keyboard move — the muscle memory of thousands of piano lessons now converted into mechanical motion. You hear, in the very structure of the music, the pattern of a human mind trying to think, trying to understand what has been done to it, and gradually, inexorably, being forced to stop thinking in any individual way and begin thinking as a component of something infinite.

The music plays for three minutes. Then it stops.

Silence falls over the chamber. It is worse than the music. In the silence, you can hear the sound of the bellows subtly expanding and contracting. You can hear the drip of preservation medium from various tubes. You can hear, distantly, a sound like voices or perhaps like wind through open spaces.

“Remarkable,” Herzfeld says softly. “The integration is proceeding perfectly.”

Investigator Fates

Captured Investigators (most likely outcome):

Each captured investigator is integrated into a specific component:

  • Katarina (Vocal Cords/Pipes): Her trachea and larynx are preserved and installed as the primary sound-producing apparatus. She retains full consciousness and can produce vocal sounds through the Engine’s pipes — but she cannot control them. The last note in Herzfeld’s journal: “Subject screaming silently, mouth moving in horror-configurations. This is acceptable. The sound produced is beautiful.”

  • Marguerite (Hands/Key Levers): Her hands and forearms are wired into the keyboard mechanism. Her fingers operate the key levers involuntarily. She can only watch as they play music she did not choose, following patterns she does not understand. She is the perfect musician, finally forced to achieve perfect technique, forever.

  • Annaliese (Lungs/Bellows): Her young, healthy lungs are installed as the primary air-pumping mechanism. She is positioned horizontally within a sealed chamber, her breathing coordinated with mechanical expansion and contraction. She is alone in darkness. She breathes forever. The loneliness is absolute.

  • Theresa (Mechanical Linkages): Her shoulders, elbows, and wrists are disarticulated and positioned to control the major mechanical systems. She moves constantly, her joints bending and straightening in patterns that produce beautiful results but serve no human purpose.

  • Johann (Diaphragm/Resonance Chamber): His ribcage is opened and his diaphragm positioned to function as the primary resonance chamber. He can feel every vibration, every resonance, every harmonic that passes through the instrument. The body itself becomes a tuning fork, vibrating at frequencies that human nervous systems were not designed to tolerate.

  • Felix (Brain/Conductor): His skull is prepared for the crown-electrode connection. His consciousness will become the Engine’s central processing unit — the mind that interprets the Canticle score and directs every component. He will gradually merge with the Engine. In the end, there will be no distinction between Felix and the instrument — he will be a mind distributed throughout a machine, still thinking, still aware, but no longer human in any meaningful sense.

Escaped Investigators:

If (unlikely) an investigator successfully escapes and reaches the surface:

  • They emerge into the Vienna night, traumatized. If they witnessed the integration process, the SAN losses should have driven them toward breaking points.
  • They face the choice of alerting the authorities — but to whom? The University itself is complicit. The local police are not equipped to handle occult investigation.
  • By the time any official investigation would begin, the Engine would be dismantled and Herzfeld and Adler would have vanished into the Aeternum Choir’s network.

The Ending

The scenario ends with Herzfeld satisfied. He reviews his journal, making notes:

“Subject One (Katarina): vocal apparatus integration 87% complete. Expected full integration within 72 hours. Consciousness degradation minimal. Subject shows no signs of rejection.”

He moves to the next restrained investigator.

“Subject Two (Marguerite): neural pathway integration proceeding ahead of schedule. The musical background appears to facilitate faster adaptation to mechanical linkage. Expected full consciousness merger within 48 hours.”

His voice is perfectly calm. He is not gloating. He is simply documenting. This makes the horror far worse — he is not excited about their suffering. He is indifferent to it.

If any investigator escaped: They are running through Vienna’s pre-dawn streets, shattered, broken, knowing what they have left behind. The city is beautiful and indifferent. No one will believe them.

Final Sanity Loss: Being an escaped survivor: 1d6/1d10 (ongoing, cumulative).

HANDOUT: THE INVITATION

(Print and hand to each player at session start)

[Sealed with wax bearing the symbol of an open measure]

Vienna, 27 March 1814

Dear Candidate,

It is with great pleasure and considerable anticipation that I write to invite you to a private audition for a musical project of unprecedented scope and artistic ambition. RUNNING THE SCENARIO: PACING & PRACTICAL ADVICE

Timing Breakdown

0:10-0:45 Act 1 Auditions, measurements, wine
0:45-1:00 Act 2a Descent through basement
1:00-1:30 Act 2b Engine revealed, Herzfeld explains

Handling Escape Attempts

  1. Act 1: Nearly impossible socially. Leaving offends Herzfeld, wastes the opportunity. Characters have every reason to stay.
  2. Act 2 (before Engine): Doors locked “for security.” Disoriented in basement. Adler blocks casually. Still possible with effort.
  3. Act 2 (after Engine): Neurotoxin actively affecting motor control. Paralyzed or semi-paralyzed. Adler hunting. Basement labyrinth. Very difficult.
  4. Act 3: Effectively impossible. Anyone who escapes should be broken, traumatized, certain the others are beyond help.

The Role of the Neurotoxin

The neurotoxin is your pacing tool and your trap:

  • Creates physical time pressure (numbness spreading, paralysis approaching)
  • Removes escape options (paralyzed characters can’t run)
  • Builds dread (each investigator watches their body failing)
  • Do not make it an instant knockout — make it progressive, visible, slow

Table Tone & Consent

Before beginning, establish boundaries. Brief players that this scenario includes: detailed descriptions of human body parts as mechanical components, consciousness of integrated victims, systematic vivisection (discussed not graphically enacted), body horror, organ horror, consciousness horror. Give players the option to decline.

AFTER THE ONE-SHOT: CAMPAIGN INTEGRATION

As Victims (Most Likely)

The six pregens integrated into the Engine become parts of the actual instrument. By August 1814 (Chapter 3), they are no longer humans — they are components:

  • Heartbreaking cameos: Main campaign investigators discover the Engine and realize the voices are conscious victims. Katarina’s voice in the pipes. Marguerite’s hands on phantom keys. Felix’s mind interpreting the score.

  • Ghosts in the machine: Victims’ personalities persist in fragmented form. The Engine occasionally “misfires” — one victim’s consciousness briefly breaks through, producing music reflecting their emotional state rather than the Canticle. Felix trying to scream through mathematics. Katarina’s voice breaking on a high note.

  • Names on missing persons lists: The six pregens appear in wanted notices, victim registries, in the despair of families searching for missing musicians.

As Escaped Survivors (If Any)

  • Broken witnesses: Carry trauma that defies explanation. Might become allies to main campaign investigators — willing to help but psychologically fragile.
  • Potential cult recruits: Isolated and disbelieved, they might be contacted by the Choir with an offer: join us, make your suffering mean something.

Thematic Integration

The one-shot’s theme of “knowing the terrible future but being unable to prevent it” mirrors the larger campaign. The pregens’ fate becomes a cautionary tale: talented, intelligent people who did everything right, and it was not enough.

TERROR REVIEW NOTES

Review 1: Horror Amplification (Completed)

  • Herzfeld’s measurements made more invasive (stethoscope, calipers, hair samples)
  • Adler studies investigators as components: “Strong wrists.” “Excellent lung capacity.”
  • Wine drinking carefully emphasized — Herzfeld monitors their trust
  • Room temperature drops (ventilation from below)
  • The Engine is moving before cloth removed — subtle breathing, wet pumping sounds
  • Brain visibly responsive to speech — it knows they’re there
  • Previous victims visible and sometimes conscious — a brief lucid moment mid-Act 3
  • Herzfeld’s final monologue is clinical notes, not triumph — indifference worse than cruelty

Review 2: Hope Elimination (Completed)

  • Basement becomes genuine labyrinth — Navigation Hard/20%, failure goes deeper not shallower
  • Adler made inescapable — supernatural knowledge of basement, tireless pursuit
  • Cult sentries with concentrated neurotoxin syringes guard upstairs exits
  • Exit doors mechanically trapped with alarms
  • Neurotoxin window narrowed to ~10 minutes from first effects to full paralysis
  • Successful escape made hollow: no one believes the survivor, authorities useless, knowledge haunts forever
  • No ending feels like victory — at best, an investigator escapes broken and alone

FINAL NOTES FOR THE KEEPER

  1. Embrace the dread, not the surprise. Your players know what’s coming. Make them feel the certainty of it.

  2. Play Herzfeld as kind. If Herzfeld is a ranting villain, the scenario becomes standard horror. If he is genuinely kind, genuinely impressed, genuinely sorry — then the horror becomes existential.

  3. Do not rush the Integration. Act 3 can feel slow. Let it. Slow = scary.

  4. Honor the victims’ consciousness. The Engine contains conscious minds. A finger twitches — that is someone. The lung expands — that is someone breathing. Make the machine human.

  5. End with Herzfeld’s notes. The final image: Herzfeld, clinical and pleased, recording that all integrations proceeded as planned. Not triumphant. Just satisfied. This is routine to him.
